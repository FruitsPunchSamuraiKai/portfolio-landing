## Project A: APAC Streaming Competitive Intelligence

**Problem:**
Global streaming competitive analysis typically focuses on paid SVOD
rivalry (Netflix vs. Disney+ vs. Prime). In APAC, this misses two
structurally important competitive forces: strong free/ad-supported
streaming ecosystems and YouTube/UGC attention fragmentation.

**Approach:**
Built a three-layer competitive framework for Japan and Korea:
- Layer 1: Premium SVOD competition
- Layer 2: Free / ad-supported streaming substitution
- Layer 3: YouTube / UGC attention competition

Scored each market on three competitive pressure dimensions (1–5)
with documented rationale. Mapped 20 platforms with pricing, content
positioning, and local strength indicators.

**Key Insight:**
Japan and Korea face fundamentally different competitive structures.
Japan's biggest pressure comes from free substitution (TVer, ABEMA) and
UGC attention fragmentation — not premium SVOD rivalry. Korea's primary
challenge is intense local premium competition (Tving, Wavve, Coupang Play).
Global frameworks that treat both markets the same will misread the most
important competitive dynamics in each.

**Why It Matters:**
Content strategy, growth planning, and investment prioritization in APAC
require market-specific competitive intelligence — not one-size-fits-all
global assumptions. This framework provides the structured market context
that content investment decisions depend on.

## Project B: Japan Content Investment Efficiency & Export Value

**Problem:**
Content investment discussions require evaluating not just how much a
title is watched, but how efficiently it generates viewing relative to
cost, and whether its value is primarily domestic or globally exportable.
Without internal financial data, how can these questions be structured
transparently?

**Approach:**
Built a content investment discussion framework using Netflix's public
Engagement Report data, IMDb metadata, and transparent category-level
cost proxies. Defined three metrics:
- Viewing Efficiency Index (viewing hours / cost proxy)
- Export Value Indicator (global engagement signal)
- Portfolio Role Classification (strategic function in slate)

Analyzed Japanese titles across four content types: anime, drama, film,
and reality/unscripted.

**Key Insight:**
Anime is the most capital-efficient content type for Japan-to-world
strategy, scoring highest on both viewing efficiency and export value.
Japanese drama serves a critical local anchor function but requires
careful title selection for export. Film is high-variance and best
approached through franchise IP. The framework's value is in structuring
the investment discussion around multiple dimensions, not producing a
single ranking.

**Why It Matters:**
Content investment cannot be evaluated by one metric alone. A framework
that separates efficiency, export value, and portfolio role enables
more nuanced slate planning discussions — exactly the kind of analysis
that strategy and finance teams need for quarterly business reviews.

## How the Two Projects Work Together

These projects address two sides of the same strategic question:

**Project A** answers: *What does the competitive environment look like?*
It maps the streaming landscape — including free and UGC competition that
global frameworks often underweight — providing the market context in which
content decisions are made.

**Project B** answers: *Given that environment, how should content investment
be discussed?* It provides a structured framework for comparing content types
on efficiency, export value, and strategic role.

Together, they demonstrate a complete analytical arc:
**market context → investment framework → strategic implications.**
